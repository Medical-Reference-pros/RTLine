📚 APRV / Bilevel — Clinical Primer
What is APRV?
APRV (Airway Pressure Release Ventilation) is an inverse-ratio, pressure-limited, time-cycled mode that keeps the lung continuously inflated at a high pressure (P-high) for most of the respiratory cycle. Brief, rapid releases to a lower pressure (P-low) allow CO₂ to be exhaled. The patient can breathe spontaneously throughout the entire cycle — both during P-high and during releases.

Think of it this way: conventional ventilation holds the lung open during inspiration then lets it deflate. APRV holds the lung open almost all the time and briefly lets it release. It is fundamentally a recruitment strategy — the sustained P-high keeps collapsed alveoli open, improving oxygenation without requiring high FiO₂.
Reserved for severe ARDS with recruitable lung — not a first-line mode
Starting values & what each one does
P-high: 25–30 cmH₂O
This is the recruitment pressure — the pressure the lung sits at for most of the cycle (during T-high). Think of it as your mean airway pressure driver and your primary oxygenation knob.
↑ P-high = more recruitment, better oxygenation, but higher plateau risk. Set it at or slightly below the plateau pressure from prior conventional ventilation. Target Vt of 4–8 mL/kg IBW per release.
P-low: 0 cmH₂O — and why
P-low is set to zero in most protocols, and this is the most counterintuitive setting in APRV. Zero does not mean the lung collapses to zero pressure. The T-low is so short (0.4–0.8 seconds) that expiratory flow is terminated before the lung can fully decompress — auto-PEEP is generated, and the lung never actually reaches P-low. Setting P-low to zero maximizes the pressure gradient during the release, driving CO₂ out rapidly in the brief window available.
When to raise P-low above zero: if the patient has high airway resistance (COPD, bronchospasm) and truly is decompressing fully during T-low, a small P-low (2–5 cmH₂O) can prevent derecruitment. Monitor with the expiratory flow-time scalar — you want the curve to not reach zero before the next release.
T-high: 4–6 seconds
The time the lung spends at P-high per cycle. This is where oxygenation happens and where the patient breathes spontaneously. Longer T-high = more time for spontaneous breathing and oxygenation, lower calculated rate.
↑ T-high = lower RR, more time for spontaneous effort (good for weaning). ↓ T-high = higher RR, more CO₂ clearance per minute. Typical starting point: 4.5 seconds.
T-low: 0.4–0.8 seconds — the most critical setting
The release time. This is the actual exhalation window, and getting it right is the key skill in APRV management. T-low must be short enough to generate auto-PEEP and prevent derecruitment, but long enough to clear CO₂.

The rule: terminate T-low when expiratory flow reaches 50–75% of peak expiratory flow rate (PEFR). You read this directly on the flow-time scalar on the ventilator screen.

↓ T-low (shorter) = more auto-PEEP, better recruitment, less CO₂ clearance per breath.
↑ T-low (longer) = more CO₂ clearance, but risks derecruitment if flow reaches zero before next P-high.
If CO₂ is too high: lengthen T-low slightly or increase T-high frequency. If oxygenation is poor: shorten T-low or raise P-high.
FiO₂: start at 0.6–1.0, wean aggressively
Because APRV dramatically improves mean airway pressure and recruitment, many patients can be weaned off high FiO₂ quickly after initiation. Wean FiO₂ to keep SpO₂ ≥92% as tolerated.
One of APRV's primary goals is reducing FiO₂ in patients who couldn't be weaned on conventional ventilation.

When APRV is failing — escalation signs
  • Persistent or worsening hypoxemia despite P-high ≥30 and FiO₂ 1.0 — lung may not be recruitable; discuss with team
  • Uncontrolled hypercapnia — if T-low adjustments and T-high rate increases fail to clear CO₂, APRV may not be appropriate for this patient
  • Patient-ventilator dyssynchrony — APRV requires the patient to tolerate the mode; severe agitation or fighting can worsen lung injury
  • Hemodynamic compromise — high mean airway pressures reduce venous return; watch for ↓ BP, ↑ HR after initiation
APRV at the bedside: The flow-time scalar is your most important monitoring tool. Open it on the ventilator screen and watch every release. You want the expiratory flow curve to be terminated (next P-high cycle to begin) when flow is still at 50–75% of peak — not at zero. Adjust T-low in 0.05-second increments until you consistently hit that target.

SBT failure criteria — stop the trial
  • SpO₂ <90% or PaO₂ <60 mmHg on FiO₂ ≤0.50
  • RR >35 bpm for more than 5 minutes — sustained tachypnea
  • HR >140 or sustained change ≥20% from baseline

Mode descriptions and clinical pearls. For tidal volume targets use the Calculator tab.
AC/VC — assist control, volume controlled
Volume-targeted · flow-limited
Delivers a set tidal volume at a set rate. Patient can trigger additional breaths, each receiving the full set Vt. Most common mode for initial intubation. Guarantees minute ventilation but can generate high pressures if compliance drops.
Go-to for initial intubation and acute respiratory failure
AC/PC — assist control, pressure controlled
Pressure-targeted · volume-variable
Delivers a set inspiratory pressure at a set rate. Tidal volume varies with compliance and resistance. Limits barotrauma risk but does not guarantee Vt.
Preferred when pressure limitation is the priority
SIMV — synchronized intermittent mandatory ventilation
Mandatory breaths + spontaneous window
Delivers mandatory breaths synchronized to patient effort, allowing spontaneous breaths between. Largely supplanted by daily SBTs for weaning.
Falling out of favor — SBT + PSV more effective for weaning
PSV — pressure support ventilation
Spontaneous mode · patient-triggered only
Augments every patient-triggered breath with a set pressure boost. No mandatory rate. PS of 5–8 cmH₂O approximates ETT resistance compensation. Apnea backup must be set.
Primary weaning mode — PS 5–8 cmH₂O for SBT
PRVC / VC+ — pressure-regulated volume control
Dual-control · volume target, pressure-limited
Targets a set Vt with the lowest necessary pressure. The ventilator measures compliance on each breath and adjusts the inspiratory pressure up or down to deliver the target volume. If compliance improves, pressure decreases automatically — and vice versa. Available under different brand names: PRVC (Maquet/Getinge Servo-i, Servo-U), VC+ (also Maquet/Getinge, same algorithm), AutoFlow (Dräger), PRVC (Hamilton), Volume Control+ (Puritan Bennett).
Volume guarantee with pressure protection — watch for Vt overshoot if compliance improves rapidly
APRV — airway pressure release ventilation
Time-cycled · pressure-limited · inverse ratio
Maintains lung recruitment at P-high for most of the cycle (T-high), with brief releases to P-low (T-low) for CO₂ clearance. Patient breathes spontaneously throughout.
ARDS with recruitable lung — use APRV tab for settings
△ High pressure alarm
  • Coughing / dyssynchrony — assess sedation, comfort, synchrony
  • Secretions / mucus plug — suction; consider bronchoscopy if persistent
  • Bronchospasm — auscultate for wheeze; administer bronchodilator
  • Pneumothorax — absent unilateral breath sounds, tracheal deviation, hypotension
  • Right mainstem intubation — absent left breath sounds; pull back ETT
  • Decreased compliance — consolidation, effusion, worsening ARDS
  • Kinked or occluded circuit — inspect tubing and water traps
△ Low pressure / low volume alarm
  • Circuit disconnect — check all connections from vent to patient
  • Cuff leak — audible leak; check cuff pressure (target 20–30 cmH₂O)
  • ETT displacement / accidental extubation — verify position; auscultate
  • Bronchopleural fistula — persistent large leak; consult surgery/pulm
● Apnea alarm
  • Over-sedation — most common; assess RASS; consider dose reduction
  • Central apnea — neurologic event; assess neuro status
  • PSV/SIMV with inadequate backup rate — verify apnea ventilation settings
  • True respiratory arrest — assess, call for help, ensure backup ventilation active

Propofol infusion syndrome (PRIS): Rare but life-threatening. Risk increases with doses >80 mcg/kg/min for >48h. Watch for: metabolic acidosis, hyperkalemia, rhabdomyolysis, renal failure, cardiac arrhythmia, lipemic plasma. Monitor triglycerides if >48h infusion.

RT pearl: Propofol causes bronchodilation — useful in bronchospasm. Also causes respiratory depression and apnea at high doses — always on mechanical ventilation.
First-line sedation for most mechanically ventilated patients — titratable, fast offset, allows daily awakening trials

💊 Analgesic infusions — analgesia-first sedation
PADIS 2018 guidelines recommend analgesia-first (analgosedation) — treat pain before adding sedation. Uncontrolled pain is the most common driver of agitation and dyssynchrony in vented patients. An RT should always ask: is this patient uncomfortable?

⚡ Neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs)
NMBAs eliminate all spontaneous respiratory effort and all patient-initiated movement. The patient is completely dependent on the ventilator. Ensure adequate sedation AND analgesia are running before and during paralysis — a paralyzed patient who is awake and in pain is a catastrophic situation. Always confirm RASS target and analgesia are addressed first.

Sugammadex reversal: 16 mg/kg IV reverses even high-dose rocuronium within 3 minutes — true emergency reversal capability. This is why many centers prefer rocuronium over succinylcholine for RSI when succinylcholine is contraindicated.

Hepatic elimination — prolonged duration in liver failure.
RSI dose (1.2 mg/kg) + sugammadex = reversible alternative to succinylcholine for difficult airway scenarios
⚡ Train-of-Four (TOF) monitoring
TOF interpretation
Train-of-four delivers four sequential supramaximal stimuli (2 Hz) to a peripheral nerve (typically ulnar nerve at wrist) and counts the number of visible or palpable twitches of the adductor pollicis (thumb).

4 of 4 twitches
<75% blockade — may be under-paralyzed
2–3 of 4 twitches
Adequate — titrate to maintain 1–2
1 of 4 twitches
✓ Target range for most indications
0 of 4 twitches
Over-paralyzed — reduce infusion rate

Monitor Q4h minimum when on continuous NMBA infusion. Document TOF count with each assessment. If 0/4 persists, hold infusion until 1–2 twitches return before resuming at lower rate.

TOF monitoring is the responsibility of nursing and pharmacy in most facilities — but RTs should know what it means and ask about it when assessing a paralyzed patient.
✅ ABCDEF Bundle — ICU Liberation
The ABCDEF bundle (also called the ICU Liberation Bundle) is an evidence-based framework that reduces duration of mechanical ventilation, ICU LOS, delirium, and mortality when implemented as a coordinated team effort. RTs are central to the A and B components.
A — Assess, prevent, and manage pain
Use validated pain scale (CPOT or NRS). Treat pain before adding sedation. Analgesia-first (analgosedation) strategy. Document pain scores Q4h minimum.
B — Both SAT and SBT
Spontaneous Awakening Trial (SAT) — daily interruption of sedation infusions. Safety screen first (no active seizures, no elevated ICP, no active agitation, not on high FiO₂/PEEP). Hold sedation and reassess.
Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT) — coordinate with SAT. Patient must pass SAT safety screen first. SAT + SBT together cut ventilator days by ~3 days vs SBT alone.
C — Choice of analgesia and sedation
Target lightest effective sedation (RASS −1 to −2). Prefer propofol or dexmedetomidine over benzodiazepines. Reassess targets each shift.
D — Delirium: assess, prevent, and manage
Screen Q12h with CAM-ICU or ICDSC. Non-pharmacologic interventions first: reorientation, day/night lighting, glasses/hearing aids, family presence, early mobility. Antipsychotics (haloperidol, quetiapine) for hyperactive delirium per order.
E — Early mobility and exercise
Begin passive range of motion immediately. Progress to active ROM, sitting at edge of bed, standing, and ambulation as tolerated — even on mechanical ventilation. PT/OT involvement from day 1. Mobility requires coordination with nursing, RT, and often two staff members.
F — Family engagement and empowerment
Family presence, open visitation, inclusion in care discussions and rounds. Family can assist with reorientation, comfort, and early mobility. Reduces patient anxiety and family PTSD.

Hypoactive delirium is the silent epidemic of the ICU — missed because it doesn’t cause problems for staff
The RT’s role in sedation management: You are at the bedside more frequently than almost anyone. You assess the patient every time you check the vent. If you notice a patient who appears undertreated for pain, over-sedated beyond their target, or showing signs of delirium — that observation belongs in your documentation and in the ear of the nurse and physician. The ABCDEF bundle is a team sport.

A note on terminology: Lung sound nomenclature has evolved and varies by institution. The ATS/ERS now recommends crackles over rales, and discourages rhonchi in favor of coarse crackles or low-pitched wheeze. You will still hear all of these terms used clinically — knowing the equivalents matters.
✓ Normal breath sounds
Vesicular
Normal peripheral lung sound
Soft, low-pitched, breezy sound heard over most of the lung fields. Inspiration is longer than expiration (3:1 ratio). The most common sound you will hear — its absence or reduction is the finding to notice.

Quality: Like a gentle breeze through leaves
Heard: Inspiration > expiration  |  Peripheral lung fields
Reduced/absent when: Pneumothorax, pleural effusion, consolidation, obesity, barrel chest, patient not taking deep enough breaths
Bronchial (tracheal)
Normal over trachea — abnormal over lung fields
Loud, high-pitched, hollow or tubular sound. Expiration is equal to or longer than inspiration. Normal directly over the trachea and manubrium — abnormal if heard over peripheral lung tissue, where it suggests consolidation transmitting central airway sounds.

Quality: Like blowing through a tube
Heard: Expiration ≥ inspiration  |  Trachea, manubrium only (normally)
If heard peripherally: Pneumonia, lobar consolidation — air replaced by fluid transmits sound better
Bronchovesicular
Normal near large airways
Intermediate sound between vesicular and bronchial. Equal inspiratory and expiratory phases. Normal over the 1st and 2nd intercostal spaces anteriorly and between the scapulae posteriorly.

Heard: I:E roughly equal  |  Upper sternal area, interscapular region
⚠️ Abnormal breath sounds
Fine crackles (formerly: fine rales)
Discontinuous  |  High-pitched  |  Late inspiratory
Brief, soft, high-pitched popping sounds heard predominantly at end-inspiration. Caused by the sudden opening of small airways or alveoli that have been held closed by surface tension or fluid.

Quality: Like walking on fresh snow, or rubbing strands of hair together near your ear
Timing: Late inspiration — if you hear them early, think coarser pathology
Clinical significance:
  • Pulmonary edema (CHF) — bilateral, dependent, gravity-dependent distribution
  • Pulmonary fibrosis — bilateral, basilar, often described as "Velcro crackles" — very characteristic
  • Early pneumonia — may be focal and unilateral
  • Atelectasis — may clear with deep breathing or coughing
Bilateral basilar fine crackles in a CHF patient that don’t clear with coughing = fluid, not secretions
Coarse crackles (formerly: rhonchi, coarse rales)
Discontinuous  |  Low-pitched  |  Early inspiratory or expiratory
Louder, lower-pitched, gurgling or bubbling sounds. Caused by air moving through secretions or fluid in larger airways. Often change or clear with coughing — a key distinguishing feature from fine crackles.

Quality: Bubbling, gurgling — like carbonated liquid or a percolating coffee maker
Timing: Early inspiration and/or expiration
Clinical significance:
  • Secretion retention — most common in intubated patients; suction and airway clearance indicated
  • Pneumonia — especially as consolidation becomes productive
  • Bronchiectasis — chronic, bilateral, often worst in the morning
  • Pulmonary edema — when severe, may produce coarser sounds
If coarse crackles clear with suctioning — that’s your answer. Secretions, not fluid.
Wheeze
Continuous  |  High-pitched, musical  |  Expiratory (primarily)
High-pitched, musical, continuous sound caused by airflow through narrowed or compressed airways. The pitch is determined by the diameter of the airway — smaller = higher pitch. Can be polyphonic (multiple pitches, diffuse obstruction) or monophonic (single pitch, focal obstruction).

Quality: Musical, whistling — like squeezing the neck of a balloon
Timing: Primarily expiratory; severe obstruction produces inspiratory wheeze too
Clinical significance:
  • Bronchospasm / asthma — diffuse, polyphonic, expiratory; responds to bronchodilators
  • COPD exacerbation — similar to asthma but with different history and baseline
  • Cardiac asthma (CHF) — wheezing caused by peribronchial edema; won’t respond to bronchodilators alone
  • Foreign body / tumor — monophonic, fixed, localized; doesn’t change with bronchodilators
  • Anaphylaxis — wheeze + stridor + systemic signs = emergency
A silent chest in a severe asthmatic is worse than a wheeze — no air movement means no sound to generate
Stridor
Continuous  |  High-pitched  |  Inspiratory  |  Heard without stethoscope
Loud, high-pitched, monophonic sound caused by partial obstruction of the upper airway — larynx, trachea, or subglottis. Typically inspiratory. Audible without a stethoscope. This is an airway emergency until proven otherwise.

Quality: Crowing, harsh, high-pitched — unmistakable once you’ve heard it
Timing: Inspiratory (upper airway obstruction); biphasic stridor (both phases) = severe obstruction
Clinical significance:
  • Post-extubation subglottic edema — most common in the ICU; racemic epinephrine, heliox, steroids
  • Croup (pediatric) — barking cough + inspiratory stridor; cool mist, racemic epi, dexamethasone
  • Epiglottitis — tripod positioning, drooling, toxic-appearing; do not agitate, call anesthesia
  • Anaphylaxis / angioedema — rapidly progressive; epinephrine immediately
  • Foreign body aspiration — sudden onset, no fever, may have witnessed event
  • Vocal cord dysfunction / paradoxical motion — inspiratory stridor, often in young females, may mimic asthma
New post-extubation stridor = call the team now. Have reintubation equipment ready.
Pleural friction rub
Discontinuous  |  Superficial, grating  |  Both phases
Grating, leathery, or creaking sound caused by inflamed pleural surfaces rubbing against each other. Heard in both inspiration and expiration, does not change with coughing. Sounds close to the stethoscope — almost as if it’s on the skin surface.

Quality: Like creaking leather, or two pieces of sandpaper rubbing together
Timing: Both inspiratory and expiratory  |  Localized
Clinical significance:
  • Pleuritis / pleurisy — associated with pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worse with inspiration)
  • Pulmonary embolism — rub + pleuritic pain + risk factors = PE until proven otherwise
  • Pneumonia with pleuritis — adjacent to infected area
  • Post-thoracic surgery — common in early post-op period
Disappears if a pleural effusion develops — fluid separates the inflamed surfaces
Egophony, bronchophony & whispered pectoriloquy
Transmitted voice sounds — consolidation signs
Voice sounds are transmitted through consolidated lung tissue more clearly than through normal aerated lung. Three tests, all looking for the same thing — consolidation:

Egophony (E to A change): Patient says "eeee" — over consolidation it sounds like "aaay." Classic finding in lobar pneumonia.

Bronchophony: Patient says "ninety-nine" — over consolidation the words are louder and clearer than over normal lung.

Whispered pectoriloquy: Patient whispers "one-two-three" — over consolidation the whisper is surprisingly clear; over normal lung it’s barely audible.

All three are positive over the same areas — consolidation, not effusion (effusion actually decreases transmitted sounds).
If you hear bronchial breath sounds peripherally + egophony = consolidation until proven otherwise
